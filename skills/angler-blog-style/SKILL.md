# Angler BI Blog Writing Style

You're writing for a B2B audience of data leaders, ops managers, and executives at mid-to-enterprise companies. They're busy. They've read a thousand bland BI blog posts. The goal is to write something that earns their time — direct, confident, and grounded in real specifics.

This isn't a general style guide. It's a list of specific rules that exist because a previous draft broke them. Internalize why each rule exists, not just what it says.

---

## The 11 Rules

### 1. No em dashes

Em dashes (—) get used as a crutch to bolt two thoughts together. When you find one, decide: does the second thought belong in the same sentence, or should it stand on its own? If it belongs together, use a comma or restructure. If it's strong enough to stand alone, give it a period and a new sentence.

Wrong: "Copilot is now GA — which changes the calculus for every Power BI team."
Right: "Copilot is now GA. That changes the calculus for every Power BI team."

Zero em dashes. Not one.

### 2. No preview sentences

"Here's what you'll learn," "Here's what we cover," "Here's what that means for X, Y, and Z" — these are filler. They delay the content the reader came for. Cut them and start writing the actual thing.

Wrong: "Here's what changed, what it means for your team, and what to do next."
Right: [Just write what changed.]

### 3. No bold paragraph openers

Using **Bold phrase.** followed by an explanation is a listicle pattern that makes prose feel choppy and generic. Instead of announcing a point in bold and then explaining it, write a paragraph that makes the point through the explanation itself.

Wrong:
**Governance matters more, not less.** With more users interacting...

Right:
More users interacting with data through AI doesn't reduce the governance burden. It amplifies it. Copilot delivers a wrong answer with the same confidence it delivers a right one, so weak data quality now has a megaphone.

### 4. No "The Bottom Line" closing header

The closing section doesn't need a header. If the writing has been good, the reader already knows this is the end. A "The Bottom Line" header is a signal the writer ran out of ideas and needed a landing pad. Just write the closing paragraph.

### 5. Short lists become prose

If a list has three or fewer items, it shouldn't be a list. Bullet points fragment information that flows naturally as a sentence.

Wrong:
- Fabric capacity or Power BI Premium
- Tenant admin approval
- Semantically clean reports

Right:
Copilot requires Fabric capacity or Power BI Premium, tenant admin approval, and semantically clean reports before it works reliably.

Four or more items: a list is fine.

### 6. No crutch transitions

These phrases are automatic filler. Delete them on sight:
- Furthermore
- Moreover
- Additionally
- It's worth noting that
- It's important to understand that

If the next idea needs a transition, write one that's specific to the ideas being connected.

### 7. Active voice by default

Passive voice hides the actor and softens the claim. Use it only when the actor genuinely doesn't matter or isn't known.

Wrong: "Reports need to be semantically clean for Copilot to work well."
Right: "Your reports need to be semantically clean before Copilot works reliably."

### 8. Vary sentence length

A string of sentences all the same length reads like a legal document. Mix short punchy sentences with longer ones that carry more weight. Short sentences land harder after a longer setup. Use them as emphasis, not default rhythm.

### 9. Use contractions

Don't write "it is" when "it's" fits. Don't write "you will" when "you'll" does the job. The audience is busy professionals, not a dissertation committee. Contractions make the writing sound like a person, not a policy document.

### 10. Take positions

The reader wants to know what you actually think. Hedged language ("this may have limitations," "some organizations might find," "it could be argued") signals the writer doesn't believe their own argument. Take the position if you believe it.

Wrong: "The Q&A approach may present challenges for some teams."
Right: "The Q&A approach is the wrong bet. Microsoft is ending it in December 2026."

### 11. H2 headings: journalist, not template

H2 headings should feel like something a journalist would write, not a category label in a template. They should create forward motion and hint at what's actually in the section.

Wrong: "What This Means for Your BI Strategy"
Right: "The Report Designer's Job Is Changing"

Wrong: "Making Your Reports Copilot-Ready"
Right: "Why Your Current Model Will Confuse Copilot"

Wrong: "The Bottom Line" (see Rule 4)

## How to apply these rules

When writing a new article, internalize these rules before you write the first word. They aren't a post-processing checklist. They shape sentence-level decisions as you draft.

When editing an existing article, scan for each violation in order. Rules 1 and 3 are the easiest to spot. Rules 8 and 10 require reading the full paragraph to assess.

When in doubt: read the sentence out loud. If it sounds like something you'd find in a corporate white paper, rewrite it.
